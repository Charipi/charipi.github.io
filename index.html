Introduction

We wanted to figure out the most important factors in MLB are the most important to winning. We specifically wanted to look at which statistics are the most important to a team's winning percentage and the effect salary has on it.

This question is extremely important to different baseball teams because every team has a set amount of money that they have to spend to create the best basbeall team possible. Kaggle - Aggregating by Team

Batting Data:

Collecting common batting statistics for each team. 'AVG' stands for batting average, the rate that a team is able to get hits. 'OBP' stands for on base percentage, the rate that a team is able to get on base, through hits, walks, or other means. 'SLG' stands for slugging, a batting average weighted by the number of bases reached each hit, rewarding more powerful hits. 'OPS' stands for on base plus slugging, the sum of on base percentage and slugging, which gives a well-rounded metric of offensive success.

In[2]:
# Batting data
team_batting = pd.read_csv('Kaggle/Batting.csv')[['teamID', 'yearID', 'AB', 'H', '2B', '3B', 'HR', 'BB', 'SO', 'IBB', 'HBP', 'SH', 'SF']].dropna().groupby(['teamID', 'yearID']).sum()
team_batting['PA'] = team_batting['AB'] + team_batting['BB'] + team_batting['SF'] + team_batting['SH'] + team_batting['HBP']
team_batting['AVG'] = team_batting['H'] / team_batting['AB']
team_batting['OBP'] = (team_batting['BB'] + team_batting['HBP'] + team_batting['H']) / team_batting['PA']
team_batting['SLG'] = (team_batting['H'] + team_batting['2B'] + 2 * team_batting['3B'] + 3 * team_batting['HR']) / team_batting['AB']
team_batting['OPS'] = team_batting['OBP'] + team_batting['SLG']

Pitching Data:

Collecting the 'ERA' statistic for each team. 'ERA' stands for earned run average and measures the average number of runs allowed by pitchers per game, not counting any defensive errors.

ERA vs Win Percentage Hypothesis Test Below

H0: There is no difference between the mean win percentage of teams with below-average (Earned Run Average) ERA and teams with above-average ERA.

HA: There is a difference between the mean win percentage of teams with below-average ERA and teams with above-average ERA.

We will use $\alpha = 0.05$.

In[7]:
from scipy.stats import ttest_ind

#Getting average ERA
avg = aggregate_teams['ERA'].mean()

#Separating teams into above average ERA and below average ERA
below_avg = aggregate_teams[aggregate_teams['ERA'] < avg]
above_avg = aggregate_teams[aggregate_teams['ERA'] > avg]

#Performing a two sample t-test
statistic, pvalue = ttest_ind(below_avg['W%'], above_avg['W%'])
print(f"T-statistic: {statistic:.3f}")
print(f"P-value: {pvalue:.3f}")

T-statistic: 15.474
P-value: 0.000

Hypothesis Test Analysis

Based on our hypothesis test and our p-value $0.000 < \alpha$, we would reject the null hypothesis. Our test shows that there is a significant difference between the win percentages of teams with above average ERA and below average ERA.

Conclusion

Linear regression showed that both runs allowed and runs scored matter equally to a team's win percentage or chance of winning the game. This shows that baseball teams should invest in both offense (batting) and defense (pitching/fielding).

Polynomial regression showed that an increase in salary has a larger effect on runs scored instead of runs allowed. Spending on offense is more valuable than spending on defense.

The random forest model showed that the best metric to predict runs scored by a team was on-base percentage (OBP) and slugging percentage (SLG). They are better predictors than traditional and more common stats like batting average. The random forest model had a $R^2$ of around 0.86 which suggests that the model is extremely effective.

Overall, baseball teams should value both offense and defense equally, but may find that spending on offense is more reliable and easier to improve with larger salaries. Furthermore, teams should evaluate players using the metrics OBP and SLG instead of other commonly used metrics as they are better indicative of how good a player is offensively.
